Introduction

This report provides a detailed statistical analysis of Care Quality Commission (CQC) ratings across different regions in England, focusing on identifying significant variations in ratings and potential biases using z-score methodology. The analysis is based on data from 5,000 CQC inspection reports conducted between January 2023 and April 2025.

The primary objective of this analysis is to identify patterns and variations in CQC ratings that may impact the interpretation of inspection outcomes, providing context for organisations to better understand their own ratings within regional and national benchmarks.

Note: This analysis is designed to provide statistical context for CQC ratings and is not intended to challenge or undermine the validity of individual inspection outcomes.

Methodology

Z-scores are used to standardize CQC ratings across different regions and inspector groups, allowing for meaningful comparisons. A z-score represents the number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean of the dataset.

Z-Score Formula

Z = (X - μ) / σ

Where:

  • Z = Z-score
  • X = Individual value (e.g., regional average rating)
  • μ = Mean (national average rating)
  • σ = Standard deviation of the dataset

Statistical significance is determined using two-tailed t-tests with the following thresholds:

  • p < 0.05: Statistically significant (95% confidence)
  • p < 0.01: Highly significant (99% confidence)

Conclusions and Recommendations

The z-score analysis of CQC ratings and policy compliance reveals several important patterns:

  1. Regional Variations: There is a statistically significant North-South divide in CQC ratings and policy compliance, with Southern regions generally receiving higher ratings.
  2. Domain Variability: The "Safe" and "Well-led" domains show the highest regional variation and lowest inspector consistency, suggesting these may be the most subjectively assessed areas.
  3. Inspector Consistency: Some inspector groups show consistent biases (either positive or negative) in their ratings, with experience level correlating with greater consistency.
  4. Policy Category Patterns: Human Resources policies show the highest compliance, while Administration and GDPR & Data Protection policies show the lowest compliance.

Recommendations:

  1. Context-Aware Interpretation: When interpreting CQC ratings, organisations should consider regional and domain-specific z-scores to understand how their ratings compare to appropriate benchmarks.
  2. Focus Areas: Organisations should prioritize improvements in the areas with lowest compliance, particularly Administration and GDPR & Data Protection policies.
  3. Inspector Awareness: CQC should consider implementing additional standardization measures for the "Safe" and "Well-led" domains to reduce subjectivity and improve consistency.
  4. Regional Support: Additional support and resources should be considered for organisations in regions with significantly lower ratings and compliance rates.

Appendix: Statistical Methodology

Data Sources

This analysis is based on data from 5,000 CQC inspection reports conducted between January 2023 and April 2025, covering care homes, domiciliary care agencies, and other adult social care services across England.

Statistical Tests

Two-tailed t-tests were used to determine the statistical significance of regional variations, with the following criteria:

  • p < 0.05: Statistically significant (95% confidence level)
  • p < 0.01: Highly significant (99% confidence level)

Categorization Criteria

Regional variation is categorized based on the range of z-scores:

  • Low: Z-score range < 1.0
  • Medium: Z-score range 1.0-1.5
  • High: Z-score range > 1.5

Inspector consistency is measured using the standard deviation of ratings:

  • High: σ < 0.5
  • Medium: σ 0.5-0.8
  • Low: σ > 0.8

Limitations

This analysis has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results:

  • The analysis does not account for variations in service types within regions
  • Historical trends over time are not fully captured in this point-in-time analysis
  • Inspector groups are anonymized and represent aggregated data rather than individual inspectors
  • Policy compliance is measured based on documentation rather than implementation
