Inspection Findings

Inspection Details: Unannounced inspection conducted on April 15, 2025, by CQC lead inspector Maria Thompson and specialist advisor Dr. James Williams (pharmacy specialist).

Safe Domain

Medication Management
"We found that medication administration records (MARs) had gaps where signatures were missing, and there was inadequate documentation of medication refusals. This created potential risks to residents as it was unclear whether they had received their prescribed medication."

Evidence: 6 medication errors documented over 3 months (March-May 2025), including 4 instances of missing signatures on MARs and 2 instances of incorrect dosage administration.

Impact: Potential medication omissions for 4 residents, though no harm was identified. Noncompliance with Regulation 12 (Safe Care and Treatment).

Risk Assessment Documentation
"Risk assessments were not consistently updated following incidents or changes in residents' needs. We found that 7 out of 15 risk assessments reviewed had not been updated within the last 3 months despite documented changes in residents' mobility and nutritional status."

Evidence: 7 out of 15 sampled risk assessments were outdated (46.7%), including 3 falls risk assessments and 4 pressure care risk assessments.

Impact: One resident experienced a fall on April 2, 2025, in circumstances that had been identified as a risk but without updated mitigation strategies.

Safeguarding Procedures
"Staff demonstrated good understanding of safeguarding procedures and could clearly articulate the process for reporting concerns. All staff interviewed could identify different types of abuse and knew how to escalate concerns appropriately."

Evidence: All 8 staff members interviewed demonstrated correct knowledge of safeguarding procedures. Records showed 3 safeguarding concerns had been appropriately reported and investigated in the past 6 months.

Impact: Effective safeguarding culture supporting resident safety and wellbeing.

Effective Domain

Staff Competency Assessment
"We found that 40% of staff did not have documented competency assessments for key clinical skills such as medication administration, moving and handling, and wound care. This meant the service could not demonstrate that all staff were skilled to undertake their roles safely."

Evidence: 8 out of 20 staff members lacked up-to-date competency assessments. Training records showed 30% of mandatory training was overdue.

Impact: Potential inconsistency in care delivery and inability to demonstrate staff competence to CQC.

Nutritional Care
"Nutritional care was generally well-managed. Residents at risk of malnutrition had appropriate care plans in place, and weight monitoring was conducted regularly. Food and fluid charts were completed accurately for those who required them."

Evidence: All 5 residents identified as at risk of malnutrition had appropriate nutritional care plans, regular weight monitoring, and accurate food/fluid charts.

Impact: Effective management of nutritional needs supporting resident health outcomes.

Well-led Domain

Quality Assurance Systems
"The service's quality assurance systems were not effectively identifying and addressing issues. We found that audits were inconsistently completed, with limited evidence of systematic follow-up on identified actions. For example, medication audits had identified missing signatures on MARs for three consecutive months, but no effective action had been taken to address this."

Evidence: 3 consecutive monthly medication audits identified the same issues without documented corrective action. No evidence of formal audit schedule or governance meeting minutes reviewing audit findings.

Impact: Failure to identify and address systemic issues leading to persistent problems. Noncompliance with Regulation 17 (Good Governance).

Staff Engagement
"Staff spoke positively about the registered manager and felt supported in their roles. Regular staff meetings were held, and staff told us they felt able to raise concerns or suggestions for improvement."

Evidence: Monthly staff meeting minutes showed active participation and open discussion. Staff survey (March 2025) indicated 85% satisfaction rate with management support.

Impact: Positive staff culture supporting continuity of care and open communication.

What Do These Statistics Mean?
Standard Deviation

What it is: A measure of how spread out data points are from the average.

In everyday terms: Shows how consistent or inconsistent CQC inspectors are in giving ratings.

How to interpret:

  • Low value (0.5-0.7): Very consistent ratings - inspectors tend to agree
  • Medium value (0.7-0.9): Moderate consistency - some variation between inspectors
  • High value (above 0.9): Inconsistent ratings - significant variation between inspectors

Why it matters: If your area has high standard deviation (inconsistent ratings), you may want to focus more on objective evidence that all inspectors would agree on.

Z-Score

What it is: Shows how unusual a value is compared to the average in a standardized way.

In everyday terms: Measures how different your region's rating patterns are from what's "normal" across England.

How to interpret:

  • -1 to +1: Normal variation - what you'd typically expect
  • +1 to +2: Unusually high variation - potentially concerning
  • Above +2: Extremely high variation - strong evidence of inconsistency
  • Below -1: Unusually consistent ratings - potentially beneficial

Why it matters: Z-scores identify regions where inspectors are significantly more or less consistent than the national average, highlighting potential bias.

Sample Distribution

What it is: The pattern or "shape" of how ratings are spread out.

In everyday terms: Shows whether ratings tend to cluster in predictable ways or follow unusual patterns.

How to interpret:

  • Normal distribution (bell curve): Natural, expected variation
  • Right-skewed: More extremely high values than expected - suggests inconsistency
  • Left-skewed: More extremely low values than expected - suggests unusual consistency

Why it matters: Unusual distributions may indicate systematic patterns in how inspectors apply standards in your region.

A pattern with a longer "tail" on the left side, showing more extremely low values than expected.

What it means:

Suggests unusual consistency in how inspectors apply ratings, with fewer inspectors giving extreme assessments.

Regions with this pattern:

East Midlands, South East (for "Requires Improvement" ratings)

Why This Matters for Quenby Rest Home

Your service is located in the East of England, which shows a normal distribution pattern. This means:

  • The ratings in your region follow expected, natural variation
  • Inspectors in your region appear to apply standards relatively consistently
  • There is a lower risk of rating bias affecting your service compared to regions like London
  • Your action plan can focus on addressing the specific issues identified, rather than needing to account for regional inconsistencies
